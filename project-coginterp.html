Do Cognitively Interpretable Reasoning Traces Improve LLM Performance?

Siddhant Bhambri*, Upasana Biswas*, Subbarao Kambhampati

* Equal contribution

School of Computing and AI, Arizona State University

NeurIPS 2025 Workshop CogInterp Workshop on Cognitively Interpretable Models of AI
Companion Paper (arXiv)

TL;DR

We fine-tune LLMs with four types of reasoning traces—R1 (distilled), summaries, post-hoc explanations, and algorithmically correct traces—and find a striking disconnect: R1-style traces produce the best task accuracy but are rated the least interpretable by humans. A 100-participant user study confirms that accuracy and human interpretability pull in opposite directions, raising fundamental questions about chain-of-thought transparency.

Motivation

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting and reasoning traces have become the dominant paradigm for improving LLM performance on multi-step tasks. But performance gains alone do not guarantee that the resulting reasoning is interpretable to end users.

We ask: Do the traces that boost accuracy the most also help humans understand the model’s reasoning? If not, what are the implications for deploying “transparent” AI systems?

Experimental Setup

Models

We use supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on four open-source LLMs:

  • LLaMA-3.2-1B-Instruct
  • LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct
  • Qwen3-1.7B
  • Qwen3-8B

Trace Types

Each model is fine-tuned on one of four trace types:

  • R1 Traces: Distilled from DeepSeek-R1—long, exploratory, often self-correcting reasoning chains typical of modern “thinking” models.
  • Summaries: Concise, paragraph-level overviews of the key steps.
  • Post-hoc Explanations: Retroactive justifications generated after the answer, designed to explain rather than derive.
  • Algorithmically Correct Traces: Manually verified step-by-step reasoning that follows a correct algorithm for the task.

Task

All experiments use the CoTemp QA benchmark—a temporal question-answering dataset requiring multi-step reasoning over timelines and events.

Key Findings: Model Performance

R1 traces consistently produce the highest accuracy across all four models (e.g., 61.9% on LLaMA-3.1-8B vs. 52–57% for other trace types). Post-hoc explanations generally yield the lowest accuracy.

  • The accuracy advantage of R1 holds across both 1B- and 8B-scale models.
  • Algorithmically correct traces—despite being logically sound—do not consistently outperform other traces, suggesting that correctness of the trace alone is insufficient for boosting model performance.

Key Findings: Human Interpretability

We conducted a between-subjects study with 100 participants recruited via Prolific (25 per trace type). Participants rated the interpretability of model-generated traces on a 7-point Likert scale.

R1 traces are rated the least interpretable (mean = 3.396), significantly lower than summaries (4.292), post-hoc explanations (4.500), and algorithmically correct traces (4.396). All pairwise comparisons are significant (Mann–Whitney U with Bonferroni correction).

  • NASA-TLX: R1 traces impose the highest cognitive workload across all dimensions—mental demand, temporal demand, effort, and frustration.
  • The interpretability–performance disconnect: The trace type that is best for the model is worst for the human. This raises a fundamental tension: optimizing for task accuracy through reasoning traces may actively harm human understanding.

Implications

Our results challenge the common assumption that showing a model’s reasoning improves transparency:

  • Faithful ≠ Interpretable: Even if R1 traces faithfully reflect the model’s internal computation, their length and self-correcting nature make them cognitively expensive for humans to parse.
  • Post-hoc explanations, while not performance-optimal, may be better suited for human-facing applications where understanding matters more than raw accuracy.
  • Future work should explore hybrid approaches—e.g., R1-style internal reasoning paired with human-friendly summary explanations.
