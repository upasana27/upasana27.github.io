About

I am a third-year PhD student in Computer Science at Arizona State University, advised by Prof. Subbarao Kambhampati. My research lies at the intersection of human–AI collaboration, multi-agent systems, and large language model (LLM)-based agents.

I develop principled computational frameworks to understand cooperation between heterogeneous agents — including humans, reinforcement learning agents, and LLM-based systems. My work combines decision-theoretic modeling, hierarchical task representations, and empirical evaluation to study adaptation, coordination, and interdependence in collaborative environments.

My long-term goal is to build rigorous foundations for human–AI multi-agent systems that are robust, interpretable, and aligned with human objectives.

Research

My research investigates how humans and AI agents can coordinate effectively under uncertainty. I focus on structured evaluation and modeling of cooperation in multi-agent systems, particularly when agents differ in reasoning capabilities, observability, and adaptability.

Research Themes

  • Human–AI Teaming and Interdependence Analysis
  • Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning
  • LLM-Based Planning and Agentic Systems
  • Hierarchical Task Representations
  • Evaluation Frameworks for Cooperative AI

Selected Research Projects

Interdependence Metrics for Human–AI Teaming

Developed a formal framework to quantify interdependencies in collaborative teams, enabling principled evaluation of zero-shot cooperation beyond task success.

LLM Planning with Hierarchical Task Networks (HTN-Bench)

Evaluating large language models on structured planning benchmarks using external verification and iterative feedback (LLM-Modulo) to improve plan correctness.

Learning to Cooperate Beyond Self-Play

Studied behavioral divergence between self-play agents and human teammates, identifying limitations of self-play training in collaborative environments.
