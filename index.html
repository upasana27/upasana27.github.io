About Me

I am a PhD student in Computer Science advised by Prof. Subbarao Kambhampati at the Yochan Lab, Arizona State University.

I study cooperation—what it means, how to formalize it, and how to build AI agents that can actually do it. Most collaborative AI is evaluated on task reward alone: if the team succeeds, the agent is called cooperative. But an agent can score high while ignoring its partner, or by forcing the human to do all the work. I’m interested in what cooperation demands beyond reward—across RL agents, humans, and LLMs.

 Teaming & Cooperation

Formalizing the structure of cooperative interaction—what makes teammates genuinely interdependent, how agents should adapt to partners who are themselves adapting, and how to benchmark different types of cooperation across computational models (Dec-POMDPs, I-POMDPs) and human teams.

 LLMs & Interaction

Understanding how LLMs reason and interact—whether their explanations actually help humans, how teams of LLM agents cooperate, and where the gaps are between model performance and human interpretability.

Research

AAAI 2026

Who is Helping Whom? Analyzing Interdependencies to Evaluate Cooperation in Human–AI Teaming

First Author · July 2024 – Present

Current ZSC agents achieve high task rewards but often fail to genuinely cooperate. We propose constructive interdependence—a STRIPS-based metric that captures how much teammates’ actions enable each other’s progress toward shared goals. A user study with 36 participants reveals that high reward does not imply cooperation.

AAMAS 2026 EA

NestRL: Nested Training for Mutual Adaptation in Human–AI Teaming

First Author · October 2025 – Present

Humans adapt to AI teammates—but most training methods ignore this. We formulate human–AI teaming as a level-2 I-POMDP and propose NestRL, a nested training regime that achieves 81.3% success vs. 54–60% for baselines when paired with adaptive partners. Validated with a 25-participant user study.

NeurIPS 2025 WS

Do Cognitively Interpretable Reasoning Traces Improve LLM Performance?

Co-First Author · 2025

SFT with DeepSeek R1 traces gives the best LLM accuracy—but a 100-participant human study reveals these traces are the least interpretable. Highlights a fundamental disconnect between what helps models and what helps users.

HRI 2024

On the Pitfalls of Learning to Cooperate with Self-Play Agents

First Author · August – December 2023

Self-play checkpoints are commonly used as proxies for diverse human skill levels, but we show that partially trained self-play agents exhibit behaviors that diverge significantly from lower-skilled humans in the Overcooked domain.
