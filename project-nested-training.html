NestRL: A Nested Training Regime for Mutual Adaptation in Human–AI Teaming

Upasana Biswas, Durgesh Kalwar, Subbarao Kambhampati, Sarath Sreedharan

School of Computing and AI, Arizona State University

AAMAS 2026 EA Under review at RLC 2026

TL;DR

Humans adapt to AI teammates, but most training methods ignore this. We formulate human–AI teaming as a level-2 I-POMDP and propose NestRL—a nested training regime where each level of agent learns against adaptive agents from the level below. NestRL achieves 81.3% success with adaptive partners (vs. 54–60% for baselines) and 79.5% delivery rate with real humans (vs. 51.8% for the strongest baseline).

Motivation

Creating RL agents that effectively collaborate with humans is a central challenge in multi-agent systems. Two fundamental limitations plague existing approaches:

  • Partner diversity ≠ adaptivity. Training against diverse but static partners (self-play, population-based) doesn’t guarantee the agent learns a truly adaptive policy. Agents may learn conformant policies that perform reasonably across partners without actually reasoning about partner type.
  • Mutual adaptation is ignored. Humans continuously adjust their behavior in response to the AI. If the robot treats the human as stationary, its actions may unintentionally induce further shifts, causing the interaction to oscillate or drift instead of converging to a shared convention.

Multi-Recipe Overcooked Domain

We ground our study in a multi-recipe Overcooked environment where a human–AI team must coordinate on one of three recipes—PotatoBroccoliSalad, LettuceOnionSalad, or TomatoCarrotSalad—without explicit communication. Each recipe requires complementary contributions from both teammates. If partners pursue different recipes, the team fails.

This setting captures two intertwined challenges: (i) multiple equilibria corresponding to distinct recipes, and (ii) mutual adaptation, where the human adjusts their behavior in response to the agent across repeated episodes within a round.

Approach: NestRL

We formalize the interaction as a finitely nested Interactive Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (I-POMDP). The key idea is bounded recursive reasoning:

  • Level-0: Fixed policies corresponding to distinct conventions (recipes). They do not model their partner.
  • Level-1: Adaptive partner policies trained against Level-0 robots. They learn to adapt to different fixed strategies, producing diverse adaptive behaviors—proxies for adaptive human teammates.
  • Level-2: The NestRL robot, trained against the set of Level-1 adaptive partners. It must reason about which adaptive partner it’s interacting with and how that partner will respond to its actions.

Theoretical Guarantee

We prove (Theorem 1) that nested training provably prevents collapse to a single coordination convention, in contrast to standard co-training where agents often converge to brittle conventions that fail to generalize.

Belief Estimation

Since the interaction is non-Markovian from each agent’s perspective, we use a latent belief embedding (GRU-based) that summarizes interaction history and approximates recursive belief updates without explicit I-POMDP inference.

Results: Simulation

NestRL achieves 81.3% average success rate with adaptive Level-1 partners, substantially outperforming LILI (60.0%), PACE (54.7%), and Generalist (54.7%). All methods achieve ~95% with non-adaptive partners, isolating the effect of nested training on adaptive coordination.

  • Mann–Whitney U tests confirm statistical significance across all comparisons (p < .02, medium effect sizes d = 0.44–0.54).
  • NestRL exhibits the lowest variability across partners (CV = 0.108 vs. 0.198–0.281 for baselines) and the smallest worst-case gap (0.266 vs. 0.333–0.400).
  • NestRL maintains 66.7–93.3% success across all adaptive partners, while baselines like PACE swing from 73.3% to 33.3%.

Convention Diversity

Standard self-play agents collapse to a single recipe convention. NestRL agents achieve success rates of 95.2%, 98.5%, and 94.7% across all three recipes, demonstrating robust coordination across multiple equilibria.

Emergent Coordination Behaviors

NestRL agents learn to wait for partner signals before committing to a recipe, then take initiative when the partner hesitates. Episodes where the agent waits achieve significantly higher success (54.9% vs. 32.1%; Mann–Whitney p < .001). These behaviors emerge naturally from nested training—they are not hard-coded.

Results: User Study (N=25)

We compare NestRL against the strongest simulation baseline (PACE) in a within-subject user study with 25 participants recruited via Prolific.

NestRL: 79.5% delivery rate vs. PACE: 51.8% (paired t(21) = 2.41, p = .025, Cohen’s d = 0.51).

  • NestRL shows an upward trend in delivery rate across episodes within a round (slope difference: t(21) = 2.87, p = .009), confirming within-round adaptation.
  • Participants report significantly lower temporal demand with NestRL (M = 4.91 vs. 5.64; t(21) = 3.84, p = .001, d = −0.82).
  • NestRL is rated as significantly more adaptive (M = 4.41 vs. 3.55; t(21) = −2.53, p = .019).
