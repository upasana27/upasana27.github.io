On the Pitfalls of Learning to Cooperate with Self-Play Agents

Upasana Biswas, Lin Guan, Subbarao Kambhampati

School of Computing and AI, Arizona State University

HRI 2024 Late-Breaking Report
ACM Paper

TL;DR

Self-play (SP) is the most widely used method for training cooperative AI agents in multi-agent settings. We show that partially trained SP checkpoints are not representative of human behavior—lower-skill SP agents diverge from lower-skill humans in systematic ways, undermining the assumption that training against SP partners prepares an agent for human teammates.

Motivation

A common approach to human–AI cooperation is to train agents via self-play and then deploy them with humans. The implicit assumption is that SP generates a distribution of partner behaviors that covers the range of human skill levels—early checkpoints resemble beginners, and later checkpoints resemble experts.

We challenge this assumption by studying whether partially-trained SP checkpoints actually reflect human behavior at corresponding skill levels in the Overcooked cooperative cooking domain.

Approach

We compare the behavioral characteristics of self-play agents at various training stages against human players at corresponding skill levels. Specifically, we analyze:

  • Action distributions: How agents allocate their time across different actions (picking up, placing, moving).
  • Spatial patterns: Which parts of the kitchen agents occupy and how they navigate the workspace.
  • Coordination strategies: Whether agents develop implicit role assignments and task-sharing conventions.
  • Error patterns: The types and frequencies of mistakes at different skill levels.

Key Findings

Self-play checkpoints diverge from human behavior most strongly at lower skill levels. While expert-level SP agents and expert humans both converge on efficient strategies, novice SP agents exhibit qualitatively different failure modes than novice humans.

  • Novice SP agents fail by executing random, uncoordinated actions. Novice humans fail by being slow or making planning errors—but their actions still reflect intentionality and spatial awareness.
  • SP agents develop idiosyncratic conventions (e.g., fixed role assignments) that no human would naturally adopt without prior coordination.
  • Training against SP partners therefore biases agents toward handling SP-style failures rather than human-style failures, leading to poor real-world generalization at lower skill levels.

Implications

These findings motivated our subsequent work on building more robust training regimes for human–AI cooperation:

  • Simply varying the training stage of SP partners is insufficient for covering human behavioral diversity.
  • Training methods should incorporate human behavioral models or diverse partner populations that capture the structured, intentional nature of human actions—even at low skill levels.
  • This work laid the groundwork for our NestRL approach, which addresses these limitations through nested training against adaptive partners modeled via I-POMDPs.
