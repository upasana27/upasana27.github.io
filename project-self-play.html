On the Pitfalls of Learning to Cooperate with Self-Play Agents

August 2023 – December 2023

Overview

This work investigates a key limitation of reinforcement learning for human–AI collaboration: agents trained via self-play often struggle to cooperate effectively with humans. While self-play has enabled major advances in multi-agent systems, it can produce coordination strategies that are brittle and incompatible with real human behavior.

Motivation

Self-play encourages agents to converge on conventions optimized for their co-trained partners. However, these conventions may be arbitrary and unintuitive to humans, leading to coordination breakdowns despite high self-play performance. We examine why this generalization gap arises and how it impacts human–robot teaming.

Approach

We analyze cooperative behavior in a collaborative task environment by comparing agents trained purely via self-play against agents trained with more diverse or human-inspired partner models. Through controlled experiments, we study how conventions emerge, how stable they are, and how well they transfer to interaction with previously unseen partners.

Key Contributions

  • Identified systematic failures of self-play agents when paired with non-self-play collaborators.
  • Demonstrated how arbitrary coordination conventions emerge during co-training.
  • Provided empirical evidence of the generalization gap between self-play and human collaboration.

Results

Our results show that strong self-play performance does not guarantee effective cooperation with new partners. Agents frequently overfit to partner-specific conventions, resulting in unstable or incompatible coordination when paired with humans or previously unseen teammates. These findings highlight the need for training paradigms that explicitly account for partner diversity and human adaptability.
